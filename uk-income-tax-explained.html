UK Income Tax Explained

Understanding UK income tax is one of the best ways to make sense of your take-home pay. Gross salary only shows what you earn before deductions. Net salary, or take-home pay, is what remains after tax, National Insurance and any other payroll deductions are taken off.

Why this page matters: many salary comparisons become much clearer once you understand what actually affects net pay. This page acts as a supporting guide for the wider salary after tax section of the site.

What affects take-home pay in the UK?

Several deductions can reduce the amount you take home from your annual salary. The main ones are:

  • income tax – tax on earnings above the relevant allowance thresholds
  • National Insurance – payroll deductions linked to employment income
  • workplace pension contributions – optional or automatic deductions depending on your setup
  • student loan repayments – if your income is high enough and you are on a repayment plan
  • other deductions – such as salary sacrifice arrangements or workplace benefits

Why gross salary is not the same as take-home pay

When someone says they earn £40,000, that is normally their gross annual salary. It does not mean they receive £40,000 into their bank account. Their actual take-home pay is lower once payroll deductions have been applied.

This is why salary after tax pages are useful. They help turn a headline salary into a more practical monthly and weekly figure.

Common deductions that people forget about

  • pension contributions through auto-enrolment
  • student loan deductions
  • changes in tax code
  • benefit-related deductions
  • salary sacrifice arrangements

These can make a noticeable difference to your final take-home pay, especially over a year.

Why monthly and weekly salary views are useful

Most people budget monthly, not yearly. That is why monthly take-home pay is often the most useful figure when comparing jobs, working out affordability or deciding whether a pay rise is worth it.

Weekly figures can also be useful if you prefer to think in smaller budgeting periods or compare income with weekly costs.
